Overview

What a cyberdeck is

Cyberdecks are generally understood in maker culture as highly customized portable computers inspired by cyberpunk fiction. Modern builds often mix compact computing hardware, custom cases, displays, keyboards, exposed wiring, antennas, and rugged styling.

This project page is structured as a template for a future build, so the content focuses on the intended direction, hardware planning, design goals, and the kind of engineering decisions the final project could involve.

Project intent

What I want to build

I want this project to become a custom portable workstation that feels more intentional and specialized than a standard laptop. The idea is to create something self-contained and unique, with a purpose-built enclosure, visible hardware character, and a layout designed around how I actually want to use it.

  • Portable screen and keyboard in one integrated body
  • Onboard power for standalone use
  • Useful external IO and expansion access
  • Mechanical and visual design that feels rugged and custom
  • Enough modularity to revise the system over time
Why it matters

Why this project is interesting

A cyberdeck is not just one component purchase or one coding task. It combines product thinking, enclosure design, hardware integration, power planning, interface decisions, and visual storytelling.

That makes it a strong personal project because it sits at the intersection of engineering, UX, fabrication, and system-level design.

Possible approach

How the build could progress

1. Concept and references

Start by locking in the overall direction: rugged field device, retro terminal, compact workstation, or a more tactical cyberpunk form factor.

2. Hardware selection

Choose the main compute platform, display size, keyboard, battery setup, storage, and any special modules like antennas, radios, sensors, or external controls.

3. Layout planning

Map where each component sits, how the internal wiring routes, which ports stay externally accessible, and how the lid, hinge, or structural shell should open and close.

4. Enclosure prototyping

Build the body through 3D printing, acrylic, aluminum, wood, or a hybrid material approach, then revise around fit, strength, and visual style.

5. Integration and testing

Assemble the system, validate thermals and power behavior, test portability, and refine cable management, controls, and user interaction.

6. Final detailing

Finish the build with labels, lighting, texture, fasteners, interface cues, and any aesthetic touches that make the device feel complete and intentional.

Hardware detail

Potential internal components

Many modern cyberdeck builds are based around a Raspberry Pi or other compact computer, usually paired with a small display, integrated keyboard, battery pack, storage, and custom wiring.

For this project, the final hardware could range from a lightweight Pi-based setup to a more powerful mini-PC build, depending on whether the goal leans more portable, more powerful, or more experimental.

Design detail

Form factor and usability

One of the most important choices will be deciding how the cyberdeck is actually meant to be used: on a desk, on the move, while standing, or as a compact field device.

That decision affects keyboard style, screen angle, handle placement, port access, hinge design, and overall ergonomics.
